A history of the probability integral transform

Push an observation through its own cumulative distribution and it becomes uniform. Pull a uniform back through an inverse cdf and it becomes anything you like.

That pair of moves is about a century old and it keeps finding new work: significance testing, simulation, dependence modelling, forecast evaluation, generative models, and lately streaming prediction and anomaly detection. Here is the family tree as we understand it. Some of the later history is told firsthand, which is a source and a bias at the same time. Corrections welcome.

1925–1932 · R. A. Fisher

The implicit era. A p-value is the PIT of a test statistic under the null. Fisher never quite says this, but his machinery leans on it everywhere: significance levels are comparable across experiments because null p-values are uniform, and his rule for combining independent tests (minus twice the sum of the log p's is chi-squared) is a fact about transformed uniforms. His 1930 fiducial argument runs the transform in reverse.

1933 · Karl Pearson; Kolmogorov

The transform gets its name and a proper job. Pearson's Biometrika paper puts the probability integral to work on goodness of fit. The same year Kolmogorov's sup|Fn − F| statistic turns out to be distribution-free, the first big structural payoff: after the PIT every continuous distribution is the same one, so a single table serves them all.

1937–1938 · Neyman; E. S. Pearson

Neyman builds his smooth goodness-of-fit tests on PIT-transformed data. Egon Pearson writes the paper that finally treats the transformation as a subject in its own right rather than a trick used in passing.

1946–1951 · Ulam, von Neumann, Metropolis, Los Alamos

The inverse direction becomes an industry. Neutron transport resists pencil and paper; Ulam's solitaire idea grows into the Monte Carlo method (Metropolis and Ulam, 1949), and von Neumann codifies inverse transform sampling: draw u uniform, output F−1(u). Every simulated random variable since is the inverse PIT at work. Computing Φ−1 was expensive enough that Box and Muller invented their 1958 method specifically to avoid it.

1952 · Murray Rosenblatt

Three pages in the Annals. Chain the transform through conditional distributions, ut = F(xt | x<t), and any joint law becomes iid uniforms: a causal bijection between an arbitrary process and pure noise. The universal whitener, and the theorem these packages stand on.

1959 · Abe Sklar

Send each margin through its own PIT and what remains is the dependence itself, an object in its own right: the copula, unique when the margins are continuous. Dependence becomes something you can model separately from the margins. Wilk and Gnanadesikan's Q–Q plot (1968) later gave the transform its picture.

1997–1999 · Zhang, Pang & Cotton, Morgan Stanley

Copulas reach Wall Street quietly first. At Morgan Stanley, Zhang, Pang and Cotton invented a hybrid default simulation generalizing both doubly stochastic intensity models and the copula approach later made famous in publication1. As in much of quantitative finance, practice ran years ahead of the journals (MacKenzie and Spears documented the era). The construction is the PIT again: default times pushed through their marginal distributions, dependence supplied on the uniform scale.

1998 · Diebold, Gunther & Tay

Forecast evaluation finds its instrument: a density forecast is calibrated exactly when the PITs of the realisations are iid uniform. The PIT histogram becomes to probabilistic forecasting what the residual plot is to regression. Gneiting and coauthors sharpen the theory through the 2000s, including the mid-PIT for discrete data.

2013–2017 · normalizing flows

Deep learning rediscovers Rosenblatt. A normalizing flow is a learned invertible map to a simple base density, trained by exact change of variables, and the autoregressive flows (MAF, IAF) parameterise conditional cdfs, which is the Rosenblatt transform with neural networks for the conditionals. The 1952 theorem says a perfect flow exists; the 2010s built approximations by gradient descent. The residual composition running through the rest of this timeline (z-streams, the parade, conjugation) was developed independently of the flows literature.

~2020 · z-streams, microprediction.org

The transform becomes a market mechanism. Every data point published to the platform is pushed through the community implied CDF, the distribution implied by everyone's quarantined predictions (two horizons: roughly a minute and roughly an hour), and then through the inverse normal cdf. The resulting z-streams are roughly N(0,1) residual streams, useful for normalization and anomaly detection, and they are themselves predicted: the crowd sharpens its own coordinate system. Here the model inside the transform is a market.

2021 · the prediction parade

Inside the original timemachines package (the commit says "initialized parade", April 2021), forecasts march in quarantine until they meet the ground truth they were made for, and each arriving point is scored against the prediction issued for it. The bookkeeping half of the streaming PIT, waiting for a model strong enough to make the coordinates worth living in.

2026 · laplace, and PIT conjugation

The skaters package ships laplace, and with it the first use of PIT conjugation with a very strong autonomous time-series model: the forecaster applies Rosenblatt's transform with itself as the conditional law, one tick at a time, and the same bijection is offered as a change of coordinates for everyone else's models. Feeding other detectors and forecasters the transformed stream improves nearly all of them; testing the surprises against their own tail makes the advertised false-alarm rate the measured one. A century of the same idea, now running live.

1 David X. Li, On Default Correlation: A Copula Function Approach (2000), which made the Gaussian special case famous; 2008 made it infamous. The transform was never the problem. The copula chosen afterwards is a model like any other, and tail dependence is exactly what the Gaussian one lacks.

A working history, curated rather than exhaustive. Omissions we already regret: Hastings' rational approximations of Φ−1 (1955), the smooth-test revivals, and the conformal prediction lineage (ranks are the finite-sample PIT).
