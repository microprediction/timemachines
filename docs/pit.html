A history of the probability integral transform

Push an observation through its own cumulative distribution and it becomes uniform. Pull a uniform back through an inverse cdf and it becomes anything you like.

That pair of moves is about a century old and it keeps finding new work: significance testing, simulation, dependence modelling, forecast evaluation, generative models, and lately streaming prediction and anomaly detection. Here is the family tree as we understand it. Some of the later history is told firsthand, which is a source and a bias at the same time. Corrections welcome.

1925–1932 · R. A. Fisher

The implicit era. A p-value is the PIT of a test statistic under the null. Fisher never quite says this, but his machinery leans on it everywhere: significance levels are comparable across experiments because null p-values are uniform, and his rule for combining independent tests is a fact about transformed uniforms: minus twice the sum of the log p-values is chi-squared. His 1930 fiducial argument runs the transform in reverse.

1933 · Karl Pearson; Kolmogorov

The transform gets its name and a proper job. Pearson's Biometrika paper puts the probability integral to work on goodness of fit. The same year Kolmogorov's sup|Fn − F| statistic turns out to be distribution-free, the first big structural payoff: after the PIT every continuous distribution is the same one, so a single table serves them all.

1937–1938 · Neyman; E. S. Pearson

Neyman builds his smooth goodness-of-fit tests on PIT-transformed data. Egon Pearson writes the paper that finally treats the transformation as a subject in its own right rather than a trick used in passing.

1946–1951 · Ulam, von Neumann, Metropolis, Los Alamos

The inverse direction becomes an industry. Neutron transport resisted pencil and paper. Ulam's solitaire idea grew into the Monte Carlo method, published by Metropolis and Ulam in 1949, and von Neumann codified inverse transform sampling: draw u uniform, output F−1(u). Every simulated random variable since is the inverse PIT at work. Computing Φ−1 was expensive enough that Box and Muller invented their 1958 method specifically to avoid it.

1952 · Murray Rosenblatt

Three pages in the Annals. Chain the transform through conditional distributions, ut = F(xt | x<t), and any joint law becomes iid uniforms: a causal bijection between an arbitrary process and pure noise. The universal whitener, and the theorem these packages stand on.

1959 · Abe Sklar

Sklar's theorem: every joint distribution is a copula applied to its margins, and the copula is unique when the margins are continuous. The proof is the PIT, applied to each margin separately, and the consequence is that dependence becomes an object you can model apart from the margins. In 1968 Wilk and Gnanadesikan gave the transform its picture, the Q–Q plot.

1997–1999 · Zhang, Pang & Cotton, Morgan Stanley

Copulas reached Wall Street quietly first. At Morgan Stanley, Zhang, Pang and Cotton invented a hybrid default simulation generalizing both doubly stochastic intensity models and the copula approach later made famous in publication1. As in much of quantitative finance, practice ran years ahead of the journals. MacKenzie and Spears later documented the era. The construction is the PIT again: default times pushed through their marginal distributions, dependence supplied on the uniform scale.

1998 · Diebold, Gunther & Tay

Forecast evaluation finds its instrument: a density forecast is calibrated exactly when the PITs of the realisations are iid uniform. The PIT histogram becomes to probabilistic forecasting what the residual plot is to regression. Gneiting and coauthors sharpen the theory through the 2000s, including the mid-PIT for discrete data.

2013–2017 · normalizing flows

Deep learning rediscovers Rosenblatt. A normalizing flow is a learned invertible map to a simple base density, trained by exact change of variables, The autoregressive flows such as MAF and IAF parameterise conditional cdfs, which is the Rosenblatt transform with neural networks for the conditionals. The 1952 theorem says a perfect flow exists; the 2010s built approximations by gradient descent. The residual composition running through the rest of this timeline (z-streams, the parade, conjugation) was developed independently of the flows literature.

2005; 2019– · conformal prediction

Machine learning rediscovers the transform in finite-sample clothing. Under exchangeability, the rank of a fresh nonconformity score among held-out ones is uniform. That is the PIT without the continuity assumption, and it is a lovely result. The field then standardized on coverage intervals and stopped short of the full predictive distribution. What gets left on the table has a name and a closed form, the residual-information gap; see Marginally Useful. A calibrated density hands you every interval at once, and the likelihood too.

~2020 · z-streams, microprediction.org

The transform becomes a market mechanism. Every data point published to the platform is pushed through the community implied CDF, the distribution implied by everyone's quarantined predictions at two horizons, roughly a minute and roughly an hour, and then through the inverse normal cdf. The resulting z-streams are roughly N(0,1) residual streams, useful for normalization and anomaly detection, and they are themselves predicted: the crowd sharpens its own coordinate system. Here the model inside the transform is a market.

2021 · timemachines package

The package emphasized online distributional residuals via the "parade" book-keeping, and was the original home for prediction algorithms since moved to skaters.microprediction.org.

2026 · laplace, and PIT conjugation

The skaters package ships laplace, the first online fully "transforms all the way down" package, and with it the first use of PIT conjugation with a very strong autonomous time-series model: the forecaster applies Rosenblatt's transform with itself as the conditional law, one tick at a time, and the same bijection is offered as a change of coordinates for everyone else's models. Feeding other detectors and forecasters the transformed stream improves nearly all of them; testing the surprises against their own tail makes the advertised false-alarm rate the measured one. A century of the same idea, now running live.
